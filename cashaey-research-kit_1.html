Research Questions

Core questions that will guide every interview, observation session, and synthesis decision. Organized by theme.

🎯

Research Goal — what we need to learn by end of Week 1

Understand how sorority chapters and student organizations currently manage money, shared resources, and informal trading — so we can evaluate whether Cashaey's community marketplace model fits naturally into their existing behavior, and identify which features matter most for a first launch.

Theme 1 · Current money & resource behavior
Q1
How does your chapter currently handle shared expenses — dues, event costs, supplies? What tools or systems do you use?
Looking for: Venmo chains, spreadsheets, chapter treasurer pain points
discovery
Q2
Think about the last time someone in your org needed something (a dress, a skill, a favor) and got it from another member instead of buying it. How did that happen?
Looking for: informal trading moments that already exist — Cashaey formalizes these
behavior
Q3
Have you ever felt like you couldn't participate in an event or activity because of cost? How did others handle that?
Looking for: financial exclusion within groups — the core problem Cashaey solves
pain point
Q4
What happens to items after big events — formal wear, decorations, props? Where do they go?
Looking for: idle inventory and secondhand habits within orgs
behavior
Theme 2 · Trust & community dynamics
Q5
How comfortable are you buying or trading something from a sorority sister vs. a stranger on Facebook Marketplace or Depop?
Looking for: trust differential inside communities — Cashaey's core advantage
trust
Q6
Have you ever lent money or covered costs for a member? Was it awkward to ask for it back? How was it resolved?
Looking for: social friction around money in tight-knit groups
trust
Q7
If your chapter had its own internal currency or credit system, how would you feel about that? What would make you trust it — or not?
Directly testing receptivity to Community Dollars concept
concept test
Theme 3 · Tech & adoption
Q8
What apps does your chapter use regularly for organizing, communicating, or paying each other? What do you wish worked better?
Looking for: existing tools, gaps, and switching triggers
competitive
Q9
When was the last time you downloaded a new app because someone in your social circle told you to? What made you actually use it?
Understanding peer adoption patterns — key for Cashaey's network-effect growth
adoption
Q10
If an exec officer or chapter president told everyone to use a new platform for trading and sharing within the chapter, would you? What would you need to feel comfortable using it?
Testing top-down adoption model — community owner = exec officer
adoption
Theme 4 · Org leadership (for exec officer interviews)
Q11
What's your biggest operational headache as an officer — the thing that takes the most time or causes the most friction?
Looking for treasurer/admin pain points that align with Cashaey's owner tools
leadership
Q12
If you could run a marketplace inside your chapter — where members list things they have, things they need, and trade — what would worry you about it? What would excite you?
Direct concept pitch reaction — use only after rapport is established
concept test
Q13
How do you currently handle situations where a member can't afford dues or event costs? Is there a formal process or is it informal?
Credit line / financial inclusion angle
leadership

TUE · Jun 17

Interview Approach & Guide

Structure, timing, rapport-building, and facilitation notes for 45-minute sessions.

👤

Participant profiles

  • · 5–8 members (general members first)
  • · 3–5 exec officers (treasurer, president, VP)
  • · Mix of chapter sizes: small (<30), medium (30–80), large (80+)
  • · At least 2 non-sorority student orgs
  • · All currently enrolled undergrads
⏱

Session format

  • · 45 min per session (60 min for officers)
  • · Video call (Zoom / Google Meet)
  • · Record with consent — transcript for synthesis
  • · 1 interviewer + optional note-taker
  • · $15–25 gift card incentive
Opening · min 0–5

Thank them for their time. State what this is: "We're exploring how communities like yours manage money and resources, and whether certain tools could help. There are no right or wrong answers — we're not testing you, we're learning from you." Ask permission to record. Start recording.

Warm-up · min 5–10 · build rapport, no pressure
  • "Tell me a bit about your chapter — how long you've been a member, what role you play."
  • "What's the best thing about being in [org name]?"
  • "How does your chapter stay organized day-to-day?" (casual, not loaded)
Core exploration · min 10–35 · use questions from Q1–Q10
  • Don't read questions robotically — use them as a conversation map.
  • Always follow "what happened?" with "how did that make you feel?" and "what would have made that easier?"
  • If they mention Venmo, Cash App, or GroupMe — go deeper. "What do you use it for specifically? What breaks down?"
  • Listen for the word "awkward" — it's a signal for a Cashaey use case.
  • For officers: shift to Q11–Q13 during this block.
Concept reaction · min 35–43 · introduce Cashaey lightly
  • "Imagine an app where your chapter had its own private marketplace — you could list things you have, ask for things you need, and use a credit system within the group. What's your gut reaction?"
  • Don't pitch. Don't defend. Listen and probe: "What specifically concerns you?" / "Who would need to be on it for you to use it?"
  • If they ask what company this is for: be honest — "We're from Cashaey, we're doing early research."
Close · min 43–45
  • "Is there anything I didn't ask about that feels relevant to how your chapter handles money or resources?"
  • "Who else in your chapter should I talk to? Could you connect us?"
  • Thank them. Send gift card within 24 hours.
🚫

Anti-patterns to avoid

  • ❌ Leading questions ("Wouldn't it be great if…")
  • ❌ Pitching or explaining features mid-interview
  • ❌ Moving on before a silence fills itself
  • ❌ Asking about hypotheticals before behavior
  • ✅ "Tell me more about that."
  • ✅ "What happened next?"
  • ✅ "Can you walk me through the last time that happened?"
  • ✅ Comfortable silence (count to 5 before speaking)

Target Org Mapping

Chapters and organizations to prioritize, their Cashaey fit, and how to get in the door.

🎯

Why sororities first

Sorority chapters are the ideal v1 community for Cashaey: they have a built-in trust network, a natural power structure (community owner = exec officers), recurring shared expenses (dues, formals, philanthropy), idle inventory (formal wear, décor), and strong peer adoption dynamics. They're also large enough for a marketplace to work but small enough to feel safe.

Tier 1 · Highest fit — target first
NPC Panhellenic

Sorority chapters at large state universities

High dues, active formals calendar, 50–200 members. Strong exec structure. Treasurer role maps perfectly to community owner.

Fit: Very High
NPHC / Multicultural Greek

BGLO sororities & multicultural chapters

Community-first values, strong mutual aid tradition, often more financially diverse membership — Cashaey's credit concept resonates deeply here.

Fit: Very High
Student Org

Pre-professional clubs (business, pre-law, pre-med)

Regular dues, conference costs, shared resources (interview suits, MCAT books). Members are analytically motivated — respond to structured tools.

Fit: High
Student Org

Cultural & international student orgs

Strong in-group trust, shared event costs (cultural shows, food), and often price-sensitive. Barter/trade is culturally familiar in many communities.

Fit: High
Tier 2 · Good fit — secondary targets
Student Org

Athletics / club sports

Shared equipment, travel costs, team gear. High trust. Smaller orgs may lack scale for a marketplace.

Fit: Medium
Student Org

Arts / performance groups

Costume sharing, instrument loans, event supply trades. Creative community — may be early adopters if UI is appealing.

Fit: Medium

Outreach Templates

Three templates for different channels and audiences. Keep it short, personal, and low-ask.

!
Golden rule of cold outreach: The first message should ask for nothing except 30 minutes of their time, in exchange for something (gift card). Never explain the whole product. Never say "app" or "platform" in the first message — say "research conversation."
Template A · Email — Chapter President or Officer
use for: exec officers · length: ~120 words
To
[First name], Chapter President / Treasurer / VP, [Chapter name]
Subject
Quick question about how [Chapter name] handles chapter finances — 30 min?
Hi [First name], I'm [Your name], doing research on how sorority chapters manage shared expenses, resources, and informal trading between members. I'd love to ask you a few questions — nothing to prepare, just a conversation. It's genuinely useful to hear from someone who runs chapter operations firsthand. It would take about 30–45 minutes over Zoom, at whatever time works for you. As a thank you, we'll send a $20 Amazon gift card after we chat. Would you be open to connecting this week or next? Thanks so much, [Your name] [Your email]
Template B · Instagram DM — General Member
use for: members · length: ~60 words · very casual
To
Active member found via chapter Instagram page
Hey [First name]! I'm doing research on how sorority sisters share, trade, and split costs with each other — stuff like who pays for what, how you handle formals, that kind of thing. Would you be open to a 30-min Zoom call? Happy to send a $15 gift card as a thank you 🙏 Totally casual, no prep needed. DM me back or I can send over a scheduling link!
Template C · Email — Student Org President (non-sorority)
use for: clubs, cultural orgs, pre-professional · ~100 words
To
President / Treasurer of [Org name]
Subject
Research on how student orgs manage money + resources — interested in talking?
Hi [First name], I'm researching how student organizations like [Org name] handle the financial and logistical side of running a group — things like dues, shared costs, gear, and how members help each other out informally. I'd love to chat with you for 30–45 minutes over Zoom. There's nothing to prepare — just a conversation about how things work in your org. We'll send a $20 gift card as a thank you. Does any time this week or next work for you? Best, [Your name]
Outreach strategy & timing
1
Send Tuesday–Wednesday, 10am–2pm local time. Emails sent in this window get ~40% higher open rates for students than evenings or weekends.
2
Personalize the first sentence. Mention something real about the chapter — a recent philanthropy event, their Instagram bio, a mutual connection. Even one line of personalization doubles response rates.
3
Follow up once, 4–5 days later. Keep it short: "Hey — just wanted to bump this in case it got buried. Still happy to chat if timing works!" Don't follow up a third time.
4
Ask for a referral in every session. "Is there anyone else in your chapter — or another chapter — I should talk to?" Snowball sampling is the fastest way to fill your interview slots in a trust-based community.
5
Target: 20 outreach contacts → 8–12 interviews by end of Week 2. That's a realistic ~40–50% response rate for a warm, personalized, gift-card-incentivized ask.
